“Butchering” of Santiago Nasar

“So he put the knife in his hand and dragged him off almost by force in search of their sister’s lost honor.” (61)
Santiago's death is the first though
“Pedro Vicario pulled out his knife with his slaughter’s iron wrist and dealt him a second thrust in almost the same place. ‘The strange thing is that the knife kept coming out clean,’ Pedro Vicario declared to the investigator. ‘I’d given it to him at least three times and there wasn’t a drop of blood.’ Santiago Nasar twisted after the third stab, his arms crossed over his stomach, let out the moan of a calf, and tried to turn his back to them.” (118)
Allusion to innocence
Not just murdered, butchered The novel uses words such as “slaughter’s iron wrist” and “calf” to show Santiago’s loss of honor of being butchered like an animal. Santiago not bleeding could show that his blood did not need to be spilled and could allude to innocence.

Naming of Pigs after Flowers

“I reminded them that the Vicario brothers sacrificed the same hogs they raised, which were so familiar to them that they called them by their names. ‘That’s true,’ one of them replied, ‘but remember that they didn’t give them people’s names but the names of flowers.’” (52)
Shows how the brothers could possibly feel remorse by needing the comfort of naming the pigs, but they are not comfortable enough giving them human names.
Also shows the nature of the people in the novel. Pigs and flowers can be replaced easily hence the naming. However, they have little regard to Santiago, a human life which they believe can be easily replaced
